An internal critique of Reason (1979)

Carter M. Smith

In this talk, I plan to:

Outline the historical context and procedure of Reason (1979) 1 .

Examine Reason’s predictive framework.

Internally critique it.

State the implications.

Begin a discussion re: these implications.

Historical context and procedure

Reason is situated in a historically important position.

In the throes of the cognitive revolution (beginning in late 1950s) 2 .

The historical context allows for an expanded research focus.

Studies inattention by gathering a descriptive account of actions-not-as-planned (ANAPs).

Reason’s explanatory leap

Reason asks:

After gathering descriptive knowledge about test failures…

(1) when do test failures occur?

If we know when, (2) can we predict which kind of test failure will occur?

Reason’s first hypothesis, formally

P1 : There exists an open-loop motor mechanism that is active when highly practiced, skilled behaviours are conducted.

P2 : There exist critical decision points , situations that rely on two or more motor programs.

P3 : If an active open-loop motor mechanism meets a critical decision point, and:

P4 : If competing motor programs have different strengths:

C : A test failure will occur.

Note a missing premise: the conducted motor program must misalign with the circumstances.

Reason’s second hypothesis, formally

P1 : If a situation allows for a test failure to occur.

P2 : The failure is determined by the motor program’s strength, such that:

P3 : The strongest erroneous motor program will win out.

C : We can predict the kind of failure by knowing motor program strength once an error is made.

Mini-discussion

Should we accept this?

I argue we should not.

A formal internal critique

P1: Assume ex ante Car < Garden; Reason’s hypothesis is supported.

P2: Assume ex ante Car > Garden; Reason’s hypothesis is contradicted.

C1 : Absent ex ante strengths, both options remain indeterminate.

P3: Reason holds that behavioural history (e.g., recency, frequency) increases a motor program’s strength.

P4: Strength is inferred post-hoc , then used for prediction.

C2 : As stated, the hypothesis is post-hoc , risking unfalsifiability.

Thus : Any predictive account must provide ex-ante measures that:

Pre-specify how behaviours (or non-behaviours!) map to strength.

Implications and discussion

How could we determine if our pre-specified criteria map onto motor program strength? Can this mapping be tested empirically ?

Could a more robust account of prior strengths allow for a purely motor explanation for test failures?

Implications and discussion

What are the methodological limitations of diary studies?

Reason states that, since ANAPs can be so functionally similar to their intended behaviour, they may go undetected for some time. Can we rely on Reason’s data as truly reflecting the base rate of ANAPs, beyond what is noticed by the participants?

What are the problems with Reason’s ANAP framework being (seemingly) unsystematic?

Implications and discussion

Reason provides two mechanistic explanations for ANAPs (CL and OL) yet also inserts agency into the equation.

Why believe that the CL, another mechanistic system, maps onto volition or free choice at all?

If mechanistic systems explain apparent volition, what does this tell us about how free our choices are?
